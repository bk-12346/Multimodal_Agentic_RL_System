--> evaluation/03-diagnose_fetch.py
- Correct pickup: 10/50 (20%)
- Wrong object picked: 5/50 (10%) — seeds 18, 26, 28, 29, 48
- Picked NOTHING (ran out of steps without grabbing anything): 35/50 (70%)

- That 70% "NOTHING" rate is the real story, and it changes the diagnosis from what I suspected. 
- This isn't primarily "the agent ignores the mission and grabs the wrong thing" — it's "the agent frequently fails to successfully navigate to and grab any object at all" within 125 steps. 
- That's a more basic skill (navigate + orient + trigger pickup at the right moment) that the policy hasn't reliably mastered yet.
- Of the 15 episodes where it actually grabbed something, 10 were correct (66.7%) — meaningfully above the 50% chance baseline for a 2-object env, even on this small a sample. 
- That's early evidence the text/fusion pathway is contributing signal, not being ignored — it just hasn't had enough training to become both reliable and accurate at the same time. 
- Low explained_variance (0.017–0.043) supports this too: the value function hasn't learned to predict returns well yet, which is consistent with "undertrained on a harder task," not "architecturally broken."
- So the fix is less about redesigning fusion and more about giving the model a fairer shot at learning the harder task.

-- trying a curriculum first — train on MiniGrid-Fetch-5x5-N1 (only 1 object, no distractor) so the agent masters navigate-and-grab in isolation
-- then fine-tune on N2 where instruction-following actually matters? 
-- That tends to converge faster and more reliably than throwing a harder task at PPO from scratch, and it'll also give us a cleaner signal about whether fusion is working, since N1 removes the "which object" ambiguity entirely.

--> evaluation/05_probe_mission_sensitivity.py
- If the action_probs rows are nearly identical across all 5 missions → the mission input is being effectively ignored by the policy, confirming a dead/weak text pathway. 
- In that case, we redesign the fusion — most likely swap the simple concat+MLP for a FiLM-style fusion (using the text embedding to modulate the vision features via learned scale/shift, a well-established technique for instruction-conditioned RL/vision-language tasks) rather than just adding more training time.
- If probs shift noticeably between missions → the signal is there but weak/noisy, and more training or a slightly larger text embedding dimension is the right fix, not an architecture change.

- Results show that the action_probs barely move at all across five completely different missions (asking for a blue key vs. a red key vs. a yellow key),
- and top_action=2 never changes. The mission input is essentially a dead signal. 
- This isn't a training-time issue anymore; it's an architecture issue.
- to further check, let's confirm the text embeddings themselves aren't collapsed to near-identical vectors 
- (a common failure mode: if gradient signal to the embedding table is weak, all rows can end up similar, especially when the only training experience that ever needed to differentiate colors was the N2 fine-tune phase, while N1 never needed language at all and could've taught the network "the text branch is irrelevant noise").

--> evaluation/06_probe_embeddings
- results show that the embeddings are not collapsed. 
- Each color has a distinct, well-separated vector (different signs, different magnitudes, norms all in a healthy 7.6–8.9 range). 
- So the language signal genuinely exists and differentiates between colors; it's not a dead/undertrained embedding table.
- This actually strengthens the FiLM diagnosis rather than changing it: the information is present, but the old concat+MLP fusion failed to make the policy use it
- the network found it easier to just ignore a clearly-informative signal and rely on vision alone, since nothing forced it to route through language.
- That's exactly the failure mode FiLM's structural gating is designed to prevent, so we retrain.

- FiLM (Feature-wise Linear Modulation): the text embedding produces a scale and shift that directly multiplies/offsets the vision features, so language isn't just "one more input the MLP might use," it's an architectural gate on vision itself.
